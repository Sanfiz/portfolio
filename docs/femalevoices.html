Pioneer Women in Climate

A glimpse into the past: the women who shaped climate science.

1819 — 1888
OVERLOOKED
E.N.F

EUNICE NEWTON FOOTE

1819 — 1888 · United States

Physicist · Pioneered greenhouse effect research

▼ open file

In 1856, Foote demonstrated that CO₂ traps heat more than any other gas — the first experimental evidence of the greenhouse effect. Because she was a woman, she was not allowed to present her own work at the AAAS meeting. A man read it on her behalf.

Three years later, John Tyndall published similar results and received full credit. Newton Foote's work went unrecognised for over 150 years. Today, average CO₂ levels are above 420 ppm. She predicted this would warm the Earth. She was right.

She also participated in the Seneca Falls Convention, fighting for women's rights. Her paper "Circumstances Affecting the Heat of the Sun's Rays" was never formally published.

FILE Nº 002
TRAILBLAZER
F.M.B

FELISA MARTÍN BRAVO

1898 — 1979 · Spain

Meteorologist · First woman at AEMET

▼ open file

First woman to earn a PhD in physics in Spain. In 1931 she passed the national exams to enter AEMET, becoming the first Spanish female meteorologist — and the only one until 1935.

She collaborated with Nobel Prize winner Charles Thomson Rees Wilson at Cambridge. The Spanish Civil War halted her career, as it did for so many women scientists. Unlike most, she was allowed to return to AEMET under the dictatorship — one of only six women employed there until the 1960s.

Between 1937 and 1940 she directed the Igeldo Meteorological Observatory, researching the violent Galernas storms of the northern Iberian coast. Between 1973 and 1974 she served as president of the Spanish Association of Meteorology — another first.

FILE Nº 003
PIONEER
J.E.L

JEAN ELIZABETH LABY

1915 — 2008 · Australia

Physicist · Atmospheric aerosols & stratosphere

▼ open file

Grew up surrounded by science — her father was a professor at the University of Melbourne. WWII redirected her path: instead of joining the Bureau of Meteorology as planned, she was assigned to produce optical instruments for the armed forces.

She worked at the University of Melbourne for over forty years. In 1959, she became the first woman to obtain a PhD in Physics in Australia. Her research covered cosmic rays, atmospheric aerosols, ozone and water vapour in the stratosphere.

In 2009, one year after her death, she was inducted into the Victorian Honour Roll of Women.

FILE Nº 004
UNSUNG
A.M

ANNA MANI

1918 — 2001 · India

Meteorologist · Ozone & weather instruments

▼ open file

A voracious reader from childhood, Mani graduated with highest honours in Physics and Chemistry. Despite years of research on spectroscopy and a completed dissertation, the Madras University denied her a PhD because she lacked a master's degree.

Undeterred, she obtained a scholarship to Imperial College London, returned to India and joined the Indian Meteorological Department. She made India self-sufficient in weather instruments — before her work, all instruments were imported from England.

She pioneered ozone research in India long before the scientific community understood its importance, developing the first Indian ozonesonde. Her story was largely unknown outside India for decades.

FILE Nº 005
RESILIENT
E.E.F

EDITH ELIZABETH FARKAS

1921 — 2000 · Hungary / New Zealand

Meteorologist · Ozone monitoring · Antarctica

▼ open file

Fled Hungary during WWII, eventually emigrating to New Zealand as a refugee in 1949. Her university qualifications were not recognised, forcing her to start over — in a language she had never formally studied.

Despite everything, she earned a Master's degree and in 1951 joined MetService, where she worked for over 35 years. She conducted world-leading ozone monitoring research for three decades using the Dobson ozone spectrophotometer.

In 1975, she became the first woman from MetService — and one of the first women ever — to set foot in Antarctica. Upon retirement she received the MetService Henry Hill Award.

FILE Nº 006
FIRST
J.S

JOANNE SIMPSON

1923 — 2010 · United States

Meteorologist · First woman PhD in meteorology (US)

▼ open file

"Clouds are a good subject for a little girl to study." — Carl Gustav Rossby, predicting Simpson would never find a job as a meteorologist if she got her PhD.

In 1949 she proved him wrong, becoming the first woman in the United States to hold a PhD in Meteorology. She was never hired as a meteorologist anyway — and dedicated her life to academia instead. She developed the first cloud model and co-proposed the Hot Towers hypothesis of tropical convection.

She authored over 190 peer-reviewed articles. She worked at NOAA and NASA for decades, eventually becoming Chief Scientist for Meteorology at NASA. In 2002 she received the International Meteorological Organization Prize — the first woman ever to do so.

FILE Nº 007
BARRIER BREAKER
J.B.B

JUNE BACON-BERCEY

1928 — 2019 · United States

Meteorologist · First African-American woman in TV weather

▼ open file

First African-American woman to earn a degree in atmospheric sciences in the US (UCLA, 1954) and one of the first to deliver weather on television. She feared appearing on air would cost her credibility as a scientist — "I didn't want to be seen as just a pretty face."

She became chief meteorologist and in 1972 received the AMS Seal of Approval — the first woman and first African-American to do so. She later spent a decade as chief administrator for Television Weather Activities at NOAA.

In 1977 she won a TV game show. She used part of her winnings to create an AGU scholarship for women studying atmospheric sciences — a legacy that outlasted her career.
